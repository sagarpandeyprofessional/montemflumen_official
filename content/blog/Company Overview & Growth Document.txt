## PAGE 2 — A Reader-Friendly Introduction (Why this document exists)

If you’ve ever moved to a new country, you know the feeling: you’re capable, motivated, and ready to build a new chapter—yet small things suddenly become hard.

## PAGE 3 — Founder Story: The Day the Mission Became Real

When I first arrived in South Korea as an international student, I expected challenges. I expected cultural differences, new routines, and a learning curve.

What I didn’t expect was **how quickly small tasks would turn into high-stress obstacles**, not because I was incapable, but because essential systems were **Korean-first** in ways that left little room for error.

### Arrival friction: when confidence meets reality

The challenge started immediately after landing.

Getting from the airport to student residence should be simple: transportation, directions, and basic questions. But in reality, it felt like walking into a world where the instructions weren’t written for you.

* communication was difficult
* directions weren’t clear in English
* even basic “what should I do next?” questions became stressful
* when you don’t have local context yet, you don’t know what’s normal or what’s risky

That first day taught me a bigger lesson: the hardest moments aren’t always dramatic—they’re the moments where you feel alone inside a system you can’t decode.

### Daily life friction: food, shopping, essentials

Soon, the challenges expanded into daily life.

Food wasn’t simply “go eat.” It was:

* where can I order confidently?
* what’s the menu?
* do I need to call?
* do they accept card?
* how do I ask for something I need?

Shopping wasn’t simply “buy essentials.” It was:

* where is the right place to go?
* which store sells what?
* how do I compare options?
* how do I trust what I’m buying?

### Paperwork friction: the highest-stakes part

The biggest challenge was paperwork.

Banking, immigration, verification, registrations—these processes are not optional. They are “system gates.” And many of them were Korean-first in ways that made mistakes easy.

Forms were complicated. Requirements felt unclear. Even when staff wanted to help, English support wasn’t always consistent. And in a system where a small mistake means delays, repeat visits, missed deadlines, or lost time—uncertainty becomes expensive.

**That experience created the thesis:**

> **Korea’s infrastructure is strong, but many interfaces are still Korean-first.**
> Foreigners don’t just need information. They need a reliable system for daily life—one that helps them complete tasks with confidence.

That is why I created **Montemflumen**—and why we built our flagship products:

* **Keasy** to unify community, guides, services, jobs, and marketplace
* **Keasy Marketplace** to bring safer discovery and trusted commerce
* **Digital Application File + English Guides** to reduce errors in high-stakes processes
* **Resume Filler** to help international residents compete more effectively

## PAGE 4 — The Real Problem: Korea’s Language Infrastructure Gap

Korea is a world-class country. The infrastructure is impressive. The public systems and private services are advanced.

But there is a structural reality that affects millions of people:

### **Many systems are Korean-first by default**

This isn’t about effort or intent. It’s about how systems evolve.

When a country grows primarily through domestic users, the default becomes:

* Korean-first instructions
* Korean-first interfaces
* Korean-first customer service workflows
* Korean-first documentation patterns

For local citizens, that’s normal. For global residents, it creates hidden friction.

### Problem A: High-friction living for foreigners

Foreign residents often experience:

* scattered information sources, inconsistent quality
* unclear process steps (“what comes first?”)
* lack of end-to-end guidance
* uncertainty about what is trustworthy
* fear of mistakes in high-stakes processes

**Example friction scenarios (illustrative):**

* “I need a phone plan and a bank account fast, but I don’t know the correct order or required documents.”
* “I visited a bank twice because I didn’t bring the right paperwork.”
* “Immigration requirements changed; I’m not sure which source is reliable.”
* “I want to join communities, but I don’t know where the right people are.”

### Problem B: Local businesses are not international-ready

Most SMEs are excellent at serving local customers. But international service requires:

* multilingual service descriptions
* clearer packaging and pricing
* reduced friction for inquiries/bookings
* trust signals (reviews, verification, profiles)
* global channel visibility

Many businesses want international customers but lack the tools to serve them reliably.

### Problem C: Existing solutions are fragmented

Users end up using multiple disconnected tools:

* meetups/events platforms
* community groups
* blogs and forums for guides
* job boards
* marketplace apps
* local directories

Fragmentation reduces trust, increases churn, and prevents any single platform from becoming “the default.”

## PAGE 5 — The Montemflumen Solution: One Ecosystem, Shared Trust

Montemflumen’s solution is not “one feature.” It’s a system.

Our strategy is:

> **One ecosystem → multiple modules → shared trust + identity layer.**

### What “ecosystem” means in practice

Instead of forcing users to jump between apps, Keasy creates continuity:

* the same identity follows you through community, guides, services, jobs, and marketplace
* your profile builds credibility over time
* your activity improves recommendations and discovery
* trust signals accumulate, not reset

### Keasy Modules (and why each exists)

**1) Events**
Because belonging is built through recurring participation—not one-time browsing.

**2) Community**
Because people don’t just need information. They need people: advice, support, and networks.

**3) Guides**
Because Korea has many “process-based” tasks that require reliable instructions.

**4) Connect (Services)**
Because foreigners don’t just need “a list”—they need *a usable service pathway*, with multilingual clarity.

**5) Jobs**
Because stability and long-term success depend on career outcomes.

**6) Marketplace**
Because daily life includes commerce: buying essentials, reselling, discovering trusted sellers.
